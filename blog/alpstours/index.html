Discover the Alps!.

PUBLISHED ON JUN 6, 2017 — MACHINE LEARNING

Before every great family vacation there comes a great family discussion: Where to go? We are mountain people. Even born and raised in northern Germany my brother and I spent 8 weeks a year, almost all our vacations, in the Alps for way over 20 years. Nothing compared to our parents (40⁄50) years. A lot of experience, a lot of opinions on where we could avoid the increasing amount of green hills in the Alps but still stay away from avalanches. After hours of discussing a minor saracastic remark: “There is a database of all ski tours over the last years. Someone can go through the site and manually build a good Excel sheet.”

“Manually - Good excel sheet.”.

I began to smile.

“Good programmer”, “Good data scientist” “Good idea” “Good bye opinion based discussion. Here comes the data science.”

Users of the website Gipfelbuch.ch are providing the relevant data. They share on which day they (attempted to) climb which mountain and some optional features like group size, summit success (no/yes) etc.

I plotted the summits on a map with leaflet and enabled dynamic filtering via Shiny, which can be seen here.

Details:

The site has no direct API so i wrote a small web crawler. Some data cleansing removed almost half the data as duplicates, leaving me with 30.000 observations over 6 years (2011-2017).

Goal: Find a valley in Switzerland to do ski mountaineering in 2018. Side conditions: Enough snow, low avalanche probability, reasonable prices, no usage of lifts.

Data exploration: R Shiny + leaflet enable a interactive visualisation of geographic data.

Sample selection: - Of course the excursions posted on the website are only a small fraction of all excursions done (may ~1-5%). - Digital literacy and tendency to persue mountain climbs are probably not very correlated. Will younger people post more often, are they fitter, take higher risks, climb higher mountains?

  • Motivation of posting tours could however depend on prestige and success of the tour.
    Prestige of tour is correlated with altitude, success with conditions. Or would you post having failed climbing a 2000m peak on a rainy day?

Filter the data: - For us the problem of decreasing amount of snow began arising in 2015. 2011-2014 are therefore expected to be poor proxies for the conditions in 2018. - To exclude summer tours, the data was filtered for the months November- April - There will be hardly any snow below 1500m. For a good ski tour you require at least 1000m climb. Summits above 3600m mostly have glaciers, which we rather avoid in winter due to the danger of cravasses. Therefore the data is filtered for summits with altitude between 2600m-3600m.
